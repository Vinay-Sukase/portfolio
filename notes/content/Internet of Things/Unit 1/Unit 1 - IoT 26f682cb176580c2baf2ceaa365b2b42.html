Introduction to IoT:

Introduction to Internet of Things

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to a paradigm in which physical objects (things) are embedded with sensors, actuators, processing capability, and network connectivity, enabling them to collect, exchange, and act upon data over the Internet without continuous human intervention.

In traditional Internet usage, humans interact with computers and mobile devices. In contrast, IoT enables machine-to-machine (M2M) communication, where devices automatically sense the environment, process information, and respond intelligently.

IoT integrates multiple disciplines such as:

  • Embedded systems
  • Wireless communication
  • Cloud computing
  • Big data analytics
  • Artificial intelligence

Diagram to draw in exam:

Sensors → Microcontroller → Communication Module → Internet → Cloud/Application

Background of Internet of Things

The conceptual foundation of IoT dates back to the 1990s, when the idea of connecting everyday objects to the Internet emerged. The term Internet of Things was coined by Kevin Ashton (1999) while working on RFID-based supply chain management.

Key technological developments that enabled IoT include:

  • Advancement in low-power sensors
  • Growth of wireless networks (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, ZigBee, LPWAN)
  • Availability of cheap embedded processors
  • Evolution of cloud platforms and big data analytics
  • IPv6 addressing, enabling billions of devices to connect uniquely

Initially, IoT was limited to industrial and RFID applications, but today it has expanded to:

  • Smart homes
  • Healthcare systems
  • Smart cities
  • Industrial automation (Industry 4.0)
  • Intelligent transportation systems

What is Internet of Things (IoT) ?

The Internet of Things (IoT) is a network of interconnected physical devices that are capable of sensing, communicating, processing, and acting based on real-world data using Internet connectivity.

Key characteristics of IoT:

  1. Connectivity – Devices are connected using Internet protocols.
  1. Sensing and Actuation – Sensors collect data; actuators perform actions.
  1. Intelligence – Data is analyzed locally or in the cloud.
  1. Automation – Minimal human intervention.
  1. Scalability – Supports billions of devices.

Examples:

  • Smart thermostats
  • Health monitoring wearables
  • Smart irrigation systems
  • Industrial sensor networks

Introduction to Embedded Systems

Embedded System

An embedded system is a dedicated computing system designed to perform a specific function within a larger system. Unlike general-purpose computers, embedded systems are task-specific, resource-constrained, and often real-time.

Components of an embedded system:

  • Microcontroller / Microprocessor
  • Memory (ROM, RAM, Flash)
  • Input devices (sensors)
  • Output devices (actuators, displays)
  • Communication interfaces

Characteristics:

  • Real-time operation
  • Low power consumption
  • High reliability
  • Compact size

Role in IoT:

Embedded systems act as the core processing units of IoT devices, handling sensor data, decision-making, and communication.

Embedded Boards

Embedded boards are hardware platforms that integrate a processor, memory, I/O ports, and peripherals on a single board. They are widely used for prototyping and deployment of embedded and IoT applications.

Examples of embedded boards:

  • Arduino boards
  • Raspberry Pi
  • ESP32 / ESP8266
  • BeagleBone

Advantages:

  • Rapid development
  • Low cost
  • Community support
  • Easy integration with sensors and networks

General Introduction (Embedded Systems in IoT)

In IoT architecture, embedded systems serve as:

  • Edge devices collecting data
  • Local processors performing preliminary analytics
  • Communication gateways sending data to the cloud

They bridge the gap between physical world signals and digital information systems.

Introduction to Arduino Boards

Arduino is an open-source embedded development platform consisting of hardware boards and software tools designed for rapid prototyping.

Key features of Arduino:

  • Based on microcontrollers (ATmega series)
  • USB programmable
  • Supports C/C++-based Arduino IDE
  • Large sensor and shield ecosystem

Why Arduino is popular in IoT:

  • Easy to interface sensors
  • Low power consumption
  • Cost-effective
  • Ideal for academic and industrial prototyping

Are Mobile Phones IoT Devices?

Mobile phones are not pure IoT devices, but they play a critical supporting role in IoT ecosystems.

Reasoning:

  • IoT devices are typically autonomous and task-specific
  • Mobile phones are general-purpose computing devices

However, smartphones can act as:

  • IoT gateways
  • Control interfaces for IoT systems
  • Data visualization tools
  • Sensor hubs (GPS, accelerometer, gyroscope)

Conclusion:

Mobile phones are IoT enablers, not IoT nodes themselves.

IoT Platforms

IoT Platforms

An IoT platform is a middleware layer that enables device management, data collection, storage, analytics, and application development.

Wearable Platforms

Wearable IoT platforms support body-worn devices such as:

  • Smartwatches
  • Fitness bands
  • Medical sensors

Characteristics:

  • Low power consumption
  • Real-time data processing
  • Continuous health monitoring

Embedded Platforms

Embedded platforms form the device layer of IoT.

Examples:

  • Arduino
  • ESP32
  • ARM Cortex-based boards

Functions:

  • Sensor interfacing
  • Local processing
  • Network communication

Cloud Platforms for IoT

Cloud platforms provide:

  • Scalable storage
  • Real-time analytics
  • Device management
  • AI/ML integration

Functions include:

  • Data ingestion
  • Visualization dashboards
  • Rule engines and alerts

What IoT Means for Developers

IoT transforms the role of developers by requiring multi-domain expertise.

Developers must understand:

  • Embedded programming
  • Networking protocols
  • Cloud services
  • Data analytics
  • Security and privacy

Challenges:

  • Scalability
  • Security vulnerabilities
  • Power optimization
  • Interoperability

Opportunities:

  • Smart city solutions
  • Healthcare innovation
  • Industrial automation
  • AI-driven IoT systems
